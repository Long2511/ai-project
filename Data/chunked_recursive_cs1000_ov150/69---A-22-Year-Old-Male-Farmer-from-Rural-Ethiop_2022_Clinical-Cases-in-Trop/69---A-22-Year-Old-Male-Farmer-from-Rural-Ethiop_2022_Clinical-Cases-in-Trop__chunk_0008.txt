Toxicity is dose-dependent, and risk factors are prolonged heavy ingestion of grass peas, malnutrition, physical exertion, concurrent illness, illiteracy, male gender and young age.

Onset occurs within 3 to 6 months of monotonous excessive grass pea consumption. Weakness that develops suddenly or on waking from sleep is a classical presentation. Stage 1 neurolathyrism presents with spastic gait and independent mobility, Stage 2 requires a cane for mobility, Stage 3 requires a crutch, and Stage 4 is characterized by the inability to bear weight with resultant contractures.

By virtue of affecting young breadwinners of rural families, neurolathyrism poses a high economic burden on poor communities in a setting where no social services or disability pensions are available.
