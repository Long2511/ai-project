In Africa the most common cause of pericardial effusion is tuberculosis; and even if there is no confirmatory evidence of tuberculosis, patients must be treated with a full course of antituberculosis treatment. In countries in central and southern Africa there is a strong association between tuberculosis and HIV infection, with over 50 per cent of tuberculosis patients being HIV positive. The advent of the HIV/AIDS epidemic in Africa was associated with a large increase in the number of patients being diagnosed with tuberculosis pericardial effusion. Once in HIV-positive patients not yet on ART, a diagnosis of pericardial effusion is made, it is important to determine whether tamponade is present, with the characteristic features being syncope, tachycardia, pulsus paradoxus and hypotension.
